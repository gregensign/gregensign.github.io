Analyzing the Air Quality in Different New York Boroughs

Natalie Leveque, Greg Ensign

This data analysis was completed at the University of Maryland - College Park, for an Introduction to Data Science class, CMSC320. This tutorial is intended to lead you through the entire data science pipeline using two different data sets. The first dataset contains air pollution data for different boroughs in New York City. The second data set contains demographic data for the five different boroughs.

Research has shown that many people are disproportionately impacted by air pollution and poor air quality, specifically those in marginalized groups such as people of color, or those who live in low income communities. Exposure to air pollution can cause a myriad of health effects, both short and long term. Among these are strokes, bronchitis, lung cancer, and other respiratory illnesses. To read more about the other adverse health effects of air pollution, click here.

The disproportionate effect that air pollution has on these minority communities makes air pollution an environmental justice issue. In this tutorial, we will be analyzing the impact that different factors have on the quantity of air pollution in the different boroughs. Specifically, we will be analyzing demographic data on race and ethnicity, as well as income data, for Brooklyn, Manhattan, Staten Island, Queens and the Bronx. Analyzing this demographic data alongside the air pollution data will help us to identify trends between these variables, and will enable us to recognize whether or not New York City's air pollution problem is also an issue of environmental justice.

In this section, we will walk you through the steps of data collection, and data cleaning.

As you can see, there are three different types of pollutants which are found in the different boroughs. These are NO2, 03, and SO2, or Nitrogen dioxide, ozone, and Sulphur dioxide.

Nitrogen dioxide is a pollutant formed by motor vehicle emissions, and is typically found in areas with lots of traffic. NO2 exposure has several potential health effects, like increased susceptibility to lung infections, and airway inflammation.

Ozone, or O3, is found in Earth's upper atmosphere, and at ground level. In the upper atmosphere, 03 protects us from UV radiation from the sun. However, at the ground level, ozone is damaging to our health. Potential health effects from ozone exposure include eye and throat irritation, as well as reduced lung function. Ozone also continues to damage lungs even when symptoms aren't present.

Sulphur dioxide is formed at power plants or industrial facilities. Exposure to SO2 can cause asthma attacks, as well as chest tightness and shortness of breath.

Longterm exposure to all three of these pollutants can have severe health effects, which is why it is so crucial that air quality is maintained at a high level. Interpretation

As we mentioned early in the tutorial, environmental justice issues are those which disproportionately affect minority or marginalized groups. As you can see from the trends above, there is a positive relationship between poverty levels and air pollution in all five boroughs, as well as between minority population and air pollution.

For the racial and ethnicity demographic data, we transformed the percentage data into a proportion of the total population of each borough. This proportion showed what percent of the total population was considered a minority or marginalized population. As you can see from the three plots, the boroughs which had lower minority populations had lower concentration of air pollution. Staten Island had the smallest minority population, and also had the lowest amount of SO2 and NO2 levels. The Bronx had a higher minority proportion, and also had higher pollution concentration. The regression line on all three of these plots showed that there is a positive relationship between minority population and pollution concentration.

We also analyzed poverty levels for each borough by calculating what percentage of each borough is in poverty, and seeing how this affects the amount of air pollution found in that borough. We found a positive relationship between poverty levels and pollution level for SO2 and NO2, however O3 had a slight negative relationship. However, for the majority of the boroughs, it was clear that higher poverty levels lead to higher pollution levels,

Both of these trends that we found in the data is indicative of an environmental justice issue present in New York City. It is unfair that certain people are subject to harmful toxins simply because of where they live or their circumstances, and are likely suffering from adverse health effects because of it.
